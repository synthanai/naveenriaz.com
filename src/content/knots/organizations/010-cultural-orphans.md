You've seen the new hire on their first Friday.
They have their laptop, their login, their wiki access.
They know how to file a ticket and order lunch.
You think they're onboarded. They're actually already leaving.

I've onboarded dozens of people just like that.
Checking off every system, every shortcut, every process.
I taught them every "how" and zero "why."
I assumed the culture would transmit itself through proximity, like WiFi. It doesn't.

When I moved to Udumelpet for school, nobody gave me an orientation packet.
But the neighbor brought food. The teacher told me a story about the founder.
Belonging requires small, deliberate acts of inclusion.
It's an oral history problem: if the elders don't tell the story, the tribe forgets who they are.

Find the person who joined your team most recently.
Don't ask them if their laptop works.
Tell them a story about the best day this team ever had.

That isolation has a name. #Cultural #Orphans.
And once you see it, you can't unsee it.

---

## The Untie

**Uproot**

New hires learn the "what" and the "how" but never the "why." Onboarding is procedural: here is your laptop, here is your Slack channel, here is the org chart. Nobody transmits the soul. The culture lives in the stories, the rituals, the unwritten rules that tenured employees absorbed through years of proximity. There is no mechanism to pass it on. Knowledge is documented. Wisdom is not.

**Navigate**

A new hire, six months in, can explain the culture to a stranger using their own words, not the company's. The "why" was transmitted through people, not documents.

**Tool**

**LIFE / Evolution**: the cultural transmission mechanism that ensures identity survives generational turnover. Evolution addresses how the soul of an organization is passed, not just the processes.

**Implement**

Pair your newest hire with a tenured person for one lunch this week. The only agenda: "Tell them a story about a time we lived our values, and a time we failed." No slides. No onboarding deck. Just a real story.

**Emerge**

When cultural transmission is deliberate, new hires reach cultural fluency in months instead of years. They stop being tourists in their own organization. Retention in the first year improves because people feel they belong to something, not just work at somewhere.
