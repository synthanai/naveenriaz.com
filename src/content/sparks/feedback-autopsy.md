A developer ships code with a subtle flaw in April. Nobody says anything. In December, their annual review mentions "attention to detail." The developer stares at the page. They cannot even remember what the reviewer is referring to.

That is not feedback. That is an autopsy: a post-mortem on events that can no longer be changed.

Deloitte's 2024 Global Human Capital Trends found that organisations moving to continuous feedback loops saw 14% higher engagement scores than those relying on annual cycles. The reason is not complicated. Feedback is most useful when the context is still fresh, when the behaviour can still be adjusted, when the person can still connect the input to the moment.

Annual reviews are comfortable for the reviewer. They are useless for the reviewed. The gap between the event and the feedback is where learning goes to die.

High-trust teams give feedback like breathing: constant, necessary, and woven into the rhythm of the work. Not saved up like ammunition for a year-end conversation that helps nobody.
