Nobody sees the culture tax on a line item. It does not show up in the budget. But it is there, in every interaction, every day.

It is the engineer who rewrites an email three times because the tone might offend the wrong person. It is the new hire who waits two weeks to ask a basic question because nobody told them it was safe to not know things. It is the meeting that runs thirty minutes over because people are talking around the real issue instead of naming it.

SHRM estimated in 2023 that toxic workplace cultures cost U.S. employers $223 billion over five years in turnover alone. But it is not just the dramatic exits. The quiet tax is worse: the friction of people navigating unwritten rules, decoding hidden meanings, and self-censoring to survive.

Healthy cultures feel frictionless. Not because conflict is absent, but because the rules of engagement are visible. People know what is safe to say. People know how disagreements get resolved.

The culture tax is invisible until you stop paying it. Then you realise how heavy it was.
