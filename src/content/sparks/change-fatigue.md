substack_article: |
  Look at the numbers. Gartner reported that employee willingness to support organizational change dropped from 74% in 2016 to a mere 43%. This decline is often misunderstood. It is not a problem of human resistance, nor is it simple apathy.
  
  The real pattern connecting this drop is exhaustion. Our teams have been through too many changes in too little time. They have dealt with the third major restructuring in two years, a new tool replacing a new tool, and strategy pivots that flatly contradicted what was presented just last quarter. Each change demanded profound emotional and intellectual energy. Yet, the return on investment for that effort has been negligible.
  
  Change fatigue is, at its core, a highly rational act of self-preservation. The workforce has simply learned that investing energy into the latest grand initiative is a statistically losing bet. They are protecting their limited bandwidth.
  
  If you are planning the next major transformation, you must change your sequence. Before launching any new system or model, you must first ensure that the last transformation has actually landed. Finish what you started. Otherwise, you are simply repeating the pattern of failure, guaranteeing the next wave of disappointment.

Gartner's 2022 research: employees' willingness to support enterprise change dropped from 74% in 2016 to 43%. Not because people hate change. Because they have been through too many changes that led nowhere.

The third restructuring in two years. The new tool that replaced the last new tool. The strategy pivot that contradicted last quarter's strategy pivot. Each one demanded emotional investment. None returned dividends.

Change fatigue is not resistance. It is rational self-preservation. The team has learned that investing energy in the latest initiative is a losing bet.

Before launching the next transformation, finish the last one.
